PRISM FANTASY: New Ways to View Light
No matter what age we live in or what country we are from, “light” has existed as an essential part of our lives.
Humans have come to understand this phenomenon in a multitude of ways from the humanities to the natural sciences.
PRISM FANTASY began with a question that blurred the line between art and science in modern times
a question of what light really is.
This exhibition features a diverse spectrum of works using natural and artificial lighting, and offers a critical view of the technological aspect of light as an art medium throughout art history. The exhibition space is divided into four themes representing
four attributes of light: reflection, infinity, spectrum, and illusion.
Center Line
PROMOTION
PRISM FANTASY offers another way to view attempts at blurring boundaries.
This exhibition has been planned to be enjoyed until the moment when the spring sunlight turns to summer sun.
There is much anticipation for this exhibition of 11 Western and Eastern artists whose works come together under the theme of light.

Lee Yongbaek
In the 1990s, artist Lee Yongbaek enthusiastically used media art, which was new to the Korean art world, and has continued to produce innovative artworks to this day.
Along with studies in international contemporary art, Lee was trained in modern art, sculpture and media art at Staatliche Akademie der Bildenden Kunste Stuttgart, Germany. Rather than being remembered as a fixed figure in art, Lee wants the world to remember him as an artist with new ideas and creative endeavors.
Using interactions, sounds, and kinetics, paintings, sculptures, and installation performances, Lee combines genres and techniques to turn social, political, and aesthetic aspects of society into concrete visualizations.
In particular, the artist has continued to present art-goers with unique social and political issues in Korean society through diverse methods.
Through the process of producing his art, Lee communicates how humans develop as they form relationships and impact each other.
In addition, he believes that we should go beyond just forming relationships and reflect deeply on our own flaws to better ourselves a process the artist believes expresses the true value of human existence and brings us happiness.

Olafur Eliasson
Olafur Eliasson is an installation artist known for his work combining different forms of art and founder of Berlin Studio Olafur Eliasson.
He was trained at the Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts where he studied high art, and he uses mostly light and other natural materials in his works.
Eliasson’s works are unique in that they raise awareness about environmental pollution by artificially positioning the viewers in “nature.”
Furthermore, the optical illusions created with light, mirrors, shadows, water, colors, and liquids combine with natural phenomenons to open new possibilities for viewer consciousness and experience.
The artist has conducted several projects in public spaces including Green River, which ran from 1998 to 2001 in cities across the world.
In 2003, Eliasson’s work represented Denmark at the 50th Venice Biennale, and he also received high acclaim for his large-scale weather project in the Turbine Hall of the London Tate Modern later that same year. By awakening the viewer’s understanding of the scale and spaces around them, Olafur Eliasson aims to awaken consciousness about each person’s part in society and subsequently take responsibility for their actions.
In this way, he believes that his art does not serve just artistic function, but also helps viewers come to a new state of recognition of scale and space.

Ryota Kuwakubo
Ryota Kuwakubo emerged as a multimedia device artist at the end of the 1990s after majoring in Plastic Art and Mixed Media at the University of Tsukuba’s School of Art and Design.
Inspired by the techniques of Bauhaus the historic German art school the artist creates his works out of diverse media including steel, plastic, film, photographs, and holograms.
Having worked briefly at a software firm, Kuwakubo has put his high aptitude in programming and software to good use as a driving force to develop new techniques and upgrade his creations.
The artist also worked collaboratively with the art unit-cum-exhibition agency Maywa Denki to co-produce BITMAN, and participating in this project prompted him to start building his own world of independent artworks.
The relationships of contrasting matter are strong across Kuwakubo’s works, including analog/digital, human/machine, and sender/receiver of information.
He also frequently uses shadows as an artistic medium in his works, which is deeply related to the artist’s understanding of existence.
Kuwakubo says that he thinks the shadow acts as an apparatus to concretely visualize the human imagination, and he has focused on creating interaction between the shadow and the inner memories of the viewer.

Gabriel Dawe
Gabriel Dawe uses sewing thread to create customized light-inspired installation works for different sites.
Having grown up under intense sunlight in Mexico’s climate, the artist began thinking about light and the sky from a young age through conversations with his parents.
He left Mexico and eventually immigrated to Texas, USA, where he grew evermore inspired by the vivid and brilliant sky filled with light.
Combining fashion and architecture, the artist created Plexus, a series that, among other things, investigates visible spectrums of light.
In the beginning stages of creation, Dawe began to contemplate clothing and architecture as well as the protective qualities of the two, which started to seep into his creations.
In particular, he employed the fundamental material for fashion thread to create architecture-based geometric structures that cease to be physically protective, yet generate an inner space of comfort and security.
These are qualities present in all of the pieces in this series.
As the series progressed, the ethereal properties of the installations made him realize that they were connected to light.
Dawe uses the entire spectrum of colored threads to create his installations in such a way that the viewers see an afterimage of a colorful blanket rather than individual threads, giving the illusion of seeing a ray of colored light frozen in space.

Bongchull Shin
Bongchull Shin is an emerging figure in the European art world who uses light and glass as his mainstay subject matter. Defining the basic attribute of glass as being its “ambiguity,” the artist has created myriad techniques to utilize the medium.
Glass has been used for many millennia as a traditional material, and through the ages it has been scientifically upgraded and wielded into a modern material.
This work embodies the opposing qualities of glass: fragility and resilience, beauty and provocativeness, the ability to divide physical spaces while also visually conjoining them.
Shin has concentrated tirelessly on these dualities found in glass. In particular, Shin focused his research on the different glass used in architecture from ancient times to modern day and the technology from each age.
Accustomed to the colors used in German Expressionist paintings and natural elements reflected in modern glass work, Shin had a chance to shake the art world with his innovative techniques. This summer, Shin will hold his first showing in Korea after having emigrated to Germany in an exhibition through which he hopes to bring the European summer to PARADISE ART SPACE.

Ivan Navarro
Ivan Navarro works with natural light and neon lights to create infinite, mesmerizing spatial art installations with socially and politically charged messages.
His works communicate issues surrounding the dictatorship of General Augusto Pinochet back in his homeland, Chile, and his experiences living abroad in American society.
The mandatory lightsout law under Pinochet that forced citizens into the dark after 5:00 p.m. awakened a craving for light in the artist.
In particular, his early works were profoundly influenced by the events of his father’s incarceration for political resistance. Navarro’s childhood was filled with ominous themes of authority, danger, and control in an oppressive hierarchical system that led him to choose his path as an artist.
The medium that oppressed and controlled the people light is repeated and expanded into the unknown, in a way that expresses both unknown aspects of life as immigrants and positive notes of hope and freedom.
The artist has been active showing works in galleries and museums across New York and London, and he also participated in the 53rd Venice Biennale.

Lee Bul
Artist Lee Bul has produced intense and striking performance art pieces, sculptures, installation pieces, paintings, and drawings since the 1980s.
Her large-scale and bulky pieces convey messages about the unreasonable social structures permeating our lives.
From the late 1990s, her artworks were exhibited at the Guggenheim Museum of New York and other prominent international art spaces, bringing Lee onto the scene as a global artist of interest.
One of her installation pieces, Majestic Splendor, incorporated the biting smell of a decomposing fish decorated with spangles a piece that won her much regard in the international art world.
Lee has also made cameo appearances in her own performance art pieces that offer sharp commentary on the view of the female body as seen through the lens of a male-dominated society.
In the late 1990s, she began to explore reality and idealizations in her Cyborg series which, in contrast to her previous works, featured sculptures with implied meanings.
Lee Bul employs unconventional materials to tackle all grades of social issues head on, posing serious conversation topics and directionality that transcend the realm of art.

Thomas Canto
Inspired by experts across a variety of industries, Thomas Canto has charged himself with the task of finding the connection between the different areas.
He is known for his work distorting speed and city structures until their original forms are effectively unrecognizable. Regardless of the genre, Canto finds inspiration from figures like the Hungarian painter and sculptor Laszlo MoholyNagy to architects like Zaha Hadid and Jean Nouvel.
His works also focus on creating images new through technology that express the relationship between humans and architecture. rgy of cities, which he is able to incorporate into his pieces thanks to his deep understanding of optical and kinetic (movement) art.
Light and movement are critical elements in modern art today, and these focal elements are clearly evident in his works. Pieces by Thomas Canto depict a fine and detailed world of the city and the interactions between humans and architecture. Containing modern components, his pieces are all accented with his unique individual flair.
Additionally, the artist considers the many reiterations of daily life in the city and works to convey the chaos of the scenes through a variety of elements working in concert.

Daniel Buren
Through the use of light and lighting fixtures, conceptual artist Daniel Buren is world renowned for his work transforming everyday architectural structures into works of art.
He started working as an artist in 1958 and joined forces with colleagues Olivier Mosset, Michel Parmentier, and Niele Toroni to form art group BMPT whose name derives from the first letters of each artist’s last name and is a main purveyor of late Abstract Minimalist painting in Paris to break down conventions of classical painting.
Believing that location can also be art, Buren works to highlight concurrent relationships by utilizing the concept of in situ, a Latin term for “original place.”
His works embody the culture and history of each location’s past and presents them in cohesive works of art.
The artist began with this concept and became driven to establish his works around the world, which led to the development of a series of works that bring out the unique characteristics of different sites in a way that effectively created a connection between art and life.
Denying all the elements in Modernism paintings as a Minimalist painter, Buren uses a standard stripe pattern as a symbolic element in his works.
His intention was to emphasize the whole rather than the part by revealing physical and architectural facets of the site, which, in turn, reduces the spaces to basic structures and holistic experience.

Jeppe Hein
Artist Jeppe Hein deconstructs conceptual art and visual images, and reconstructs them into minimalist installation art pieces.
He combines his own area of art in collaboration with experts in engineering, architecture, and other fields to complete his works.
In particular, he mainly focuses on creating interactive pieces by using light and mirrors.
In fact, Hein designs site-specific installations to make his art something that can only be fully experienced in a specific location.
Hein works to open the possibility for viewers to feel through different facets of his pieces, contemplating the relationship between the location and each piece and a sense of change.
There are often unexpected interactions and dialogs between the viewer and the works, which are the precise moments that the artist works to elicit.
In this exhibition, Jeppe Hein continues to show his major works using light and mirrors that blur the boundaries between concepts/matter and reality/illusion.
On the first floor, two of his pieces also combine with the other works using light and prisms as part of their own, unifying the disparate pieces.

Daniel Rozin
Daniel Rozin is an iconic media artist who creates works that depict what he sees in the mirror.
He completed his first work, Easel, in 1997 and has continued to perfect his interactive art with the public over the past 22 years.
Using a combination of cameras, screens and mechanics, Rozin’s works are completed only when the movement of viewers in front of the works is added.
If we were to say that the traditional exhibition space was a one-way transfer of images to the viewer, these works by Daniel Rozin are uniquely interactive in that the viewers directly influence the final images that manifest in the works as they are being viewed.
In the Mechanical Mirrors series, the artist explores human movement and representation by reflecting changes in movement onto the flat surfaces of the works. While working on his projects, Daniel Rozin has focused on the relationship of the viewer and his artworks.
Seeing their own reflection as light on the surface of an object and observing how that image transforms, they are afforded many opportunities to look back on themselves.
It also led him to consider new perspectives of time and space in regards to his existence, as sparked by his works.
Through this exhibition, Rozin suggests an antipodal view of his works that lends to a new perspective on life.
